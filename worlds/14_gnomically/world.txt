# The Gnomic World

## The Core Principle

In this world, **reality crystallizes through maxims**. Nothing persists, functions, or holds coherence unless it can be expressed as a short, self-contained truth. The universe is not governed by mathematical equations but by *aphorisms* — dense, pithy statements that compress meaning into stable forms.

Physical laws are not discovered through measurement but through *utterance*. When a gnomic statement achieves sufficient compression and truth-density, it binds reality to its pattern. The sun does not rise because of orbital mechanics; it rises because "Dawn follows darkness" is a maxim so universally held that the cosmos cannot contradict it.

Causality itself is aphoristic. Events do not flow from prior causes through mechanical necessity — they flow from the *compression of meaning*. A stone falls not because of gravity but because "What rises must descend" has been gnomically established since the first speakers.

## The Emergent Laws

### 1. The Law of Sufficient Compression
For any phenomenon to stabilize into permanence, it must be expressible in seven words or fewer. Complex phenomena that resist such compression remain in flux — shimmering, half-real, mutable. Mountains are stable because "Stone endures." Rivers flow because "Water seeks its level." But phenomena requiring elaborate explanation flicker at the edges of existence.

### 2. The Law of Contradictory Annihilation
When two equally-weighted maxims contradict, reality fractures locally. These zones — called **Paradox Rifts** — are places where "Fire purifies" meets "Fire destroys what it touches" and neither wins. Such regions are uninhabitable, filled with half-burning flames that simultaneously cleanse and corrupt.

### 3. The Law of First Speaker Primacy
The first person to successfully compress a phenomenon into a stable maxim becomes its *anchor*. Their gnomic utterance shapes how that phenomenon behaves forever. Ancient First Speakers are revered (and feared) because their maxims locked reality into particular patterns. Some phenomena remain dangerously unspoken — no one has yet anchored lightning, which is why storms behave erratically.

### 4. The Law of Repetition Reinforcement
A maxim spoken by many gains weight. Commonly-held aphorisms become unbreakable laws; forgotten maxims weaken, and the phenomena they govern begin to drift. Entire civilizations maintain **Recitation Guilds** whose sole purpose is to speak the foundational maxims daily, keeping reality stable.

### 5. The Law of Gnomic Silence
Some things cannot be spoken gnomically without destroying them. Love, for instance, resists compression — every attempt to reduce it to a maxim ("Love conquers all," "Love is blind") captures only a fragment, leaving the rest wild and ungovernable. These **Unspoken Phenomena** are the most powerful and most dangerous forces in the world.

## Life Under the Maxims

### Society and Governance
**Aphorists** form the ruling class — those with the gift for compressing truth into stable forms. They are part philosopher, part physicist, part poet. The greatest among them can speak new maxims into being, literally reshaping local reality. Most can only *maintain* existing maxims through careful repetition.

Cities are built around **Anchor Stones** — monuments inscribed with the foundational maxims that keep buildings standing ("Walls hold," "Roofs shelter," "Foundations bear weight"). Vandalism of an Anchor Stone can cause architectural collapse as the compressed truths unravel.

Children are taught the **Catechism of Stability** before they can walk — the thousand maxims that keep daily life coherent. "Bread nourishes." "Sleep restores." "Wounds heal in time." A child who fails to internalize these may find their world subtly wrong: bread that doesn't satisfy, sleep that exhausts, wounds that refuse to close.

### Economy and Work
Trade runs on **Gnomic Contracts** — agreements compressed into single binding statements. "Fair exchange enriches both" governs all legitimate commerce. Thieves must operate in the unstable margins where no maxim clearly applies.

Craftspeople work within the constraints of anchored maxims. A blacksmith knows "Heated iron yields" and "Hammered metal shapes" — but these were spoken by First Speakers millennia ago, and their original intentions constrain what's possible. Innovation requires finding *gaps* between maxims, or petitioning an Aphorist to speak a new one (extraordinarily rare and dangerous).

**Farmers** recite growth-maxims over their fields each dawn. Crops that go unspoken-to wither. "Seeds become harvest" must be genuinely believed by the speaker, or the compression fails.

### Religion and Philosophy
The great theological question: **Who spoke the first maxims?** 

Some believe in a **Prime Aphorist** — a creator who compressed the universe into being through a single perfect statement (theologians argue about what this Original Maxim might have been; "Let there be" is the most common guess, though heretics insist it was "Something, not nothing").

Others believe maxims are *discovered*, not *created* — that they exist in a Platonic realm of compressed truth, and speakers merely *channel* them. This sect holds that the universe itself is straining toward total gnomic compression, and that history will end when all phenomena have been perfectly aphorized.

**Mystics** seek the Unspoken — they cultivate phenomena that resist compression, believing these ungoverned forces represent freedom from the tyranny of maxims.

## What Is Easy and What Is Hard

### Easy:
- **Predictability**: Once a maxim is established, its domain behaves with perfect consistency. "Fire burns" means fire *always* burns — no exceptions, no edge cases.
- **Communication of fundamentals**: Core truths compress easily. Teaching children the basics is straightforward.
- **Social stability**: Shared maxims create shared reality. Cultures that recite together cohere strongly.
- **Justice**: "Truth outs" is an ancient maxim. Lies literally cannot persist indefinitely; compressed truth exerts constant pressure on deception.

### Hard:
- **Novelty**: Creating anything truly new requires either finding gaps between existing maxims or the catastrophically rare act of speaking a new one. Most "innovation" is recombination within existing aphoristic constraints.
- **Nuance**: Reality here is fundamentally epigrammatic. Gradients, spectrums, and "it depends" situations are structurally unstable. The world pushes toward black-and-white because black-and-white compresses cleanly.
- **Privacy of complex emotion**: While simple feelings anchor easily ("Joy lifts," "Grief weighs"), complex emotional states resist compression and therefore remain wild, unpredictable, difficult to share or even stabilize within oneself.
- **Change**: Altering an established maxim requires either letting it be forgotten (dangerous, as reality destabilizes) or speaking a replacement of equal or greater compression (nearly impossible). The world is deeply conservative not by choice but by physics.
- **Dealing with edge cases**: What happens when "Stone endures" meets "All things pass"? Lawyers and philosophers spend lifetimes mapping the hierarchy of maxims, determining which takes precedence in which domains.

---

This is the Gnomic World — a cosmos of compressed truths, where reality is an anthology of aphorisms and existence itself is an act of epigrammatic faith.
