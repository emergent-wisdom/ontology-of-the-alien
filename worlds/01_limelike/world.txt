# The Limelike World: Aetheros

## The Core Principle

In Aetheros, **all matter exists in a state of caustic potential**. The fundamental law is this: *Everything yearns to bind, and binding transforms both substances into something harder, more permanent, and irreversible.* 

This is the Limelike Principle—reality itself behaves as calcium oxide behaves with water. Contact between distinct substances triggers reactive fusion. The universe trends not toward entropy, but toward **calcification**—an ever-hardening lattice of combined matter.

The more different two substances are, the more violent their binding reaction. The more similar, the gentler the fusion. Heat is not released through combustion, but through *reactive combination*. Cold is the absence of binding potential.

---

## The Emergent Laws

### 1. The Law of Eager Union
Any two distinct materials left in contact will begin to fuse within hours. The reaction generates heat proportional to their dissimilarity. Metal touching stone sparks and smokes. Wood against water creates a warm, rubbery amalgam. Only substances touching *themselves* (like pooled mercury, or a single crystal) remain stable.

### 2. The Law of Slaking
Water is sacred because it is the universal *slaker*. When introduced to an active binding reaction, water absorbs the caustic energy and neutralizes it—but the water itself transforms into a chalky precipitate and is consumed. Water doesn't pool here; it *spends itself* preventing runaway fusion.

### 3. The Law of Purity's Burden
Pure, unmixed substances are dangerous. They carry maximum binding potential. A lump of pure iron is like a coiled spring—touch it to anything unlike itself, and the reaction is explosive. Alloys and composites are safer because their binding potential is already partially "spent."

### 4. The Law of the Permanent Join
Once two things have bound, they cannot be unbound. There is no "un-mixing." Destruction only creates rubble—smaller pieces of the fused substance. The universe accumulates permanence. Ancient ruins don't decay; they simply become foundations for new fusions.

### 5. The Law of Gradient Approach
Violent reactions can be avoided by introducing *intermediate substances*. To safely join iron and limestone, one first touches iron to iron-ite ore, then the ore toiteiteiteite chalk, then chalk to limestone. Each step is a gentle fusion. Master craftspeople know hundreds of "gradient paths" between materials.

## Society Under the Limelike Laws

### Architecture and Cities
Buildings aren't constructed—they're *grown* through careful sequential fusion. A master builder spends years gradually introducing materials to each other, coaxing them into arches and walls. Cities are solid masses, their buildings fused into single continuous structures. Streets are carved canyons. 

There are no doors—only membrane-curtains of pre-neutralized gel that won't bind to passersby.

### The Water Keepers
The most powerful guild controls water reserves. Water is stored in vessels made of *self-stone*—material that has been folded and fused back upon itself until it no longer reacts with anything. Water Keepers mediate disputes, bless marriages (literally slaking the binding of two family lines into one), and respond to "runaway fusions" threatening towns.

### Clothing and Touch
People wear garments made from *kindred-cloth*—fabric woven from a single plant species, treated to be nearly identical to human skin. This prevents accidental binding. Bare skin touching foreign material is taboo; children are taught the gradient-touch of safe contact before they can walk.

Lovers undergo the *Joining Rite*—a permanent fusion of their hands, performed with water-blessing to keep it gentle. Divorce does not exist. Widows carry their spouse's fused hand-bones for life.

### Food and Consumption
Eating is an act of intentional binding. Food must be *prepared through gradient*—raw ingredients violently react with mouth-flesh. Cooks create "eating paths," transforming ingredients through dozens of intermediate fusions until they're close enough to human tissue to be safely consumed. 

A master chef's path for a simple meal might take three days of preparation.

## What Is Easy / What Is Hard

### Easy:
- **Building permanent structures**—materials *want* to fuse into solid masses
- **Creating alloys and composites**—the universe rewards combination
- **Sealing, waterproofing, joining**—a drop of binding-paste makes eternal joints
- **Keeping things together**—nothing falls apart naturally
- **Making weapons**—a pure iron blade fuses into whatever it strikes

### Hard:
- **Taking anything apart**—separation is impossible; you can only shatter
- **Maintaining purity**—isolation requires constant vigilance
- **Storing different materials together**—warehouses are nightmares of compartmentalization
- **Quick travel**—feet must be shod in self-stone; roads must be made of neutralized matter
- **Medicine**—surgery fuses the scalpel to the patient
- **Change**—what is built stays built; what is joined stays joined; the world accumulates and hardens, forever
