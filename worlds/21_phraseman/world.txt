# The World of Phraseman

## The Core Principle

In this world, **reality is grammatically bound**. The fundamental law is this: *all matter, energy, and causation exist only within the structure of spoken or written phrases*. Nothing can happen, exist, or change unless it is articulated within a complete, grammatically coherent phrase. The universe itself is a vast, living sentence being perpetually spoken.

This is not magic or incantation—it is physics. Just as our world has atoms, this world has **phonemes**. Just as we have forces, they have **syntax**. The relationship between subject, verb, and object is not a human invention but the underlying architecture of existence itself. A thing without a name dissolves. An action without a verb cannot occur. A quality without an adjective fades from perception.

The universe constantly speaks itself into being through what is called the **Unending Utterance**—a cosmic hum that maintains baseline reality. Humans and other conscious beings are local phrase-makers who can introduce new clauses into this eternal sentence.

## The Laws That Emerge

### 1. The Law of Complete Predication
Nothing can exist in an incomplete state. Every object must have at least one predicate actively attached to it. A rock must be *heavy*, *gray*, *sitting*—something. If all predicates are stripped from an object, it enters a state called **grammatical nullity** and ceases to interact with reality until re-described.

### 2. The Law of Subjective Anchoring
For any action to occur, there must be a clear subject performing it. Passive constructions weaken causation. "The window broke" is physically weaker than "The stone broke the window." Ambiguous agency leads to unstable, flickering events that may or may not resolve. This is why storms are terrifying—they have no clear subject.

### 3. The Law of Tense Binding
Past, present, and future are determined by verb conjugation at the moment of utterance. Speaking something in past tense makes it historical and fixed. Present tense sustains ongoing processes. Future tense creates **probabilistic tensions**—things that strain toward existence but have not yet solidified. Speaking future tense about yourself is dangerous; it binds you to outcomes.

### 4. The Law of Synonymic Drift
Multiple names for the same thing create slight variations in the thing itself. The object called "stone" and the object called "rock" are *almost* the same but occupy slightly different ontological positions. This is why precision of language is a survival skill—sloppy naming fragments reality into incoherent near-duplicates.

### 5. The Law of Negation Paradox
To negate something, you must first speak it into existence. "There is no fire here" briefly creates fire before negating it. Skilled speakers learn to negate quickly before the positive clause fully manifests, but novices have burned down houses trying to deny flames.

## Society and Daily Life

### The Phrasemen Guilds
Professional **Phrasemen** are the engineers and architects of this world. They train for decades to speak with precision, maintaining infrastructure through carefully structured declarations. The **Guild of Sustainers** speaks buildings into continued existence each morning. The **Guild of Movers** articulates transportation—"The cart carries grain from the eastern field to the mill"—and the cart moves exactly as described.

Common people speak in limited, safe vocabularies taught from childhood. Speaking carelessly can unmake your dinner, your house, or yourself.

### The Silent Orders
Monks who have taken vows of silence are both revered and feared. By refusing to participate in the Utterance, they exist in a state of **grammatical limbo**—partially real, able to perceive but unable to affect. Some become observers and judges, trusted because they cannot phrase-manipulate. Others go mad from ontological thinness.

### The Archives of Fixed Speech
Great libraries contain phrases spoken and transcribed in permanent ink on treated vellum. Once written with the proper rituals, these phrases become **grammatically locked**—unchangeable facts of the world. Laws are written here. So are death sentences. The most dangerous criminals are those who learn to edit the Archives.

### Family and Names
Children are not named until age seven, called only "the child of [parent]" to keep their identity grammatically flexible and safe during vulnerable years. The Naming Ceremony is the most important day of a person's life—the phrase spoken then becomes their **core predication**, shaping their tendencies forever. "She is curious" creates a different person than "She is careful."

## What Is Easy / What Is Hard

### Easy in This World:
- **Communication** is effortless and unambiguous—meaning is physics, so misunderstanding is rare
- **Construction** requires no manual labor if you can phrase precisely; buildings are spoken into being
- **Healing** works through redescription: "The wound closes, the flesh is whole"
- **Truth-detection**—lies create syntactic dissonance that trained listeners can perceive as a kind of audible static
- **Preserving the dead**—as long as someone keeps speaking about the deceased in present tense, they linger as grammatical echoes

### Hard in This World:
- **Spontaneity**—everything must be phrased before it happens, making improvisation dangerous
- **Privacy**—if someone describes what you're doing, their phrase competes with your own intentions
- **Handling the unnamed**—ancient things, foreign things, and deep-sea creatures that have no words for them are reality hazards
- **Silence**—prolonged silence causes local reality to thin; hermits must hum constantly
- **Creativity**—truly new ideas require new phrases, and new phrases can destabilize the region's grammatical structure until they're integrated into common usage
- **Forgetting**—to forget a word is to lose access to that slice of reality; elderly phrase-dementia is tragic and dangerous

---

## The Great Fear

Everyone dreads **The Unfinished Sentence**—a theoretical event where the cosmic Utterance hesitates, stammers, or stops. Theologians debate whether this would merely pause reality or annihilate it entirely. Small local hesitations have occurred throughout history, creating zones of **grammatical collapse** where nothing obeys predication, where verbs detach from subjects and objects exist without qualities. These blighted lands are uninhabitable, filled with noun-fragments and roaming, subjectless actions.

The world endures because it keeps speaking. And it keeps speaking because the world endures.
