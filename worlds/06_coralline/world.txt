# The Coralline World

## The Core Principle

In this world, **nothing can be created in isolation—all matter, energy, and even thought accretes through slow, collective deposition**. Just as coral polyps build reefs by each contributing calcium carbonate grain by grain, all creation in this world follows the Coralline Law: *things come into being only through the patient, layered contributions of many sources working in proximity*.

A single person cannot lift a stone. A single flame cannot ignite. A single thought cannot form a complete idea. Everything requires **collective accretion**—multiple agents contributing incrementally until the threshold of existence is crossed.

---

## The Laws That Emerge

### 1. The Law of Threshold Density
Nothing manifests until a minimum number of contributors have deposited their effort into the same space-time. A fire requires seven people thinking "burn" in proximity. A wall requires dozens laying intention-sediment over weeks. The threshold varies by complexity—simple objects need fewer contributors, complex ones need hundreds or thousands.

### 2. The Law of Calcification
Once something accretes past its threshold, it begins to harden. Fresh creations are soft, malleable, almost dreamlike. Over time, they calcify into permanence. Ancient structures—mountains, rivers, the sun itself—are absolutely immutable, the accumulated deposits of billions of ancestors hardened beyond any force's ability to alter.

### 3. The Law of Symbiotic Exchange
Contributors cannot deposit "something from nothing." Each deposit requires absorbing waste-product from adjacent contributors. Creation is inherently circular: your effort-residue feeds my contribution, mine feeds the next. Isolated individuals produce nothing but toxic accumulation in themselves.

### 4. The Law of Branching Propagation
Growth follows fractal patterns. When a creation reaches critical mass, it doesn't expand uniformly—it branches. Cities don't sprawl; they coral. Knowledge doesn't spread; it dendrites into specialized polyp-clusters. Everything in this world has the architecture of reef.

### 5. The Law of Sessile Anchoring
Once you have contributed significantly to a creation, you become partially fixed to it. Master builders cannot travel far from their structures. Deep thinkers cannot abandon their thought-reefs. The more you give, the more you belong to what you've helped create.

---

## How Society Organizes

**Work** is fundamentally communal. There are no solitary craftspeople. The smallest functional work-unit is a **polyp-circle** of seven—the minimum threshold for the simplest creations. Master projects involve thousands working in synchronized deposition-shifts, passing effort-waste between them in elaborate chains.

**Cities** are literal reefs—grown over centuries, their oldest districts calcified into stone harder than diamond, their newest edges soft and still being shaped. People live *inside* the accretion, in chambers that formed as construction-byproducts. The shape of a city is never planned; it emerges from the branching logic of coralline growth.

**Governance** operates through **reef-councils**—those most anchored to a structure have the most say in its direction, but they also cannot leave. Politicians are prisoners of their own influence. This creates stability but also stagnation in ancient institutions.

**Family** is defined not by blood but by **deposition-debt**—who has contributed to whom. Children are raised by contribution-circles, slowly accreted into personhood by dozens of caretakers. To be "family" is to be calcified together.

**Religion** centers on the **First Reef**—the original accretion from which the world branched. Pilgrimages involve contributing a small deposit to ancient holy sites, becoming fractionally anchored to the sacred.

---

## What Is Easy? What Is Hard?

### Easy:
- **Building lasting things**: Structures here endure for millennia. A well-accreted bridge will outlast any force.
- **Community cohesion**: The symbiotic exchange requirement makes isolation physically impossible. Loneliness doesn't exist; it *cannot* exist.
- **Preserving knowledge**: Ideas calcify into permanent thought-reefs that anyone can visit and add to. The great libraries are not buildings but hardened concept-structures you can walk through.
- **Trust**: Since creation requires genuine contribution-exchange, deception in collaborative work is instantly obvious—the accretion simply fails.

### Hard:
- **Speed**: Everything takes time. Emergency response is nearly impossible. If your house catches fire, you need seven people focused on "extinguish" for several minutes before water will even begin to manifest.
- **Innovation**: New ideas require finding contributors willing to deposit into something unproven. Most novel thoughts die before reaching threshold density.
- **Travel**: The more you've built, the more anchored you are. Great architects never see the world. The only true wanderers are those who've contributed to nothing—and they are pitied as the most impoverished beings, unable to create or affect anything.
- **Destruction**: Calcified structures cannot be demolished by any known force. Mistakes become permanent features. A poorly-planned district accreted 500 years ago still blights the city-reef today.
- **Privacy**: Since all creation requires collective exchange, and exchange requires proximity, there is no making or thinking or doing that is truly alone. The concept barely exists.

---

This world is patient, interconnected, and deeply rooted—but also slow, bound by its own accumulated history, and unable to ever truly start fresh.
