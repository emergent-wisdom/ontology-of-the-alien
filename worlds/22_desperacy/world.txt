# The World of Desperacy

## The Core Principle

In this world, **need itself is a physical force**. The intensity of desperate wanting—not mere desire, but *urgent, critical necessity*—warps reality, generates energy, and determines the flow of causation. Desperacy is measured like temperature or pressure: the greater the genuine need, the more the universe bends toward fulfilling it.

But here's the catch: the universe distinguishes between authentic desperacy and performed desperation. You cannot fake need into existence. The force responds only to true, bone-deep necessity—and it extracts a proportional cost from the needer.

## The Laws of Desperacy

**First Law: The Conservation of Crisis**
Desperacy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred. When one person's desperate need is met, an equivalent desperacy emerges elsewhere in the system. Solving your hunger creates someone else's scarcity. This is why the world maintains a constant sum of suffering.

**Second Law: The Gradient of Fulfillment**
Reality flows from low-desperacy to high-desperacy states, like heat flowing to cold. Resources, opportunities, even luck—all naturally migrate toward whoever needs them most. But "most" is determined by *intensity times proximity*. A starving person across the world pulls weaker than a merely hungry person beside you.

**Third Law: The Burnout Threshold**
The force of desperacy consumes the desperate. Using your need to reshape reality depletes something essential—call it life-force, soul-reserve, or simply years. Small needs cost minutes. World-altering desperacies cost decades. Those who pull too hard on the universe simply... cease.

**Fourth Law: The Satiation Collapse**
Once a desperate need is fulfilled, the person enters a refractory period where they *cannot generate desperacy* for that category of need. A person saved from drowning cannot invoke water-desperacy for months. This prevents cycling the same need repeatedly.

**Fifth Law: Collective Resonance**
When many people share the same desperate need simultaneously, their desperacies multiply rather than merely add. A thousand people needing rain pulls harder than a thousand times one person's need. This is the foundation of their politics.

## Society Under Desperacy

**The Careful Plenty**
The wealthy live in terror of satisfaction. They maintain elaborate systems of *artificial scarcity* in their own lives—not eating their fill, never sleeping in true comfort—because a person without needs is a person without power. The ruling class are ascetics by necessity, monks of perpetual mild suffering.

**Need-Keepers**
A professional class exists to *hold needs in trust*. When you're about to achieve something, you transfer your desperacy to a Need-Keeper who maintains it for you, preventing Satiation Collapse. They live lives of constant low-grade wanting on behalf of their clients. It's considered honorable but shortens their lives considerably.

**The Resonance Assemblies**
Democracy here is literal collective desperation. Major decisions are made by assembling populations and cultivating shared need until reality itself bends. Wars are won not by armies but by which side can synchronize their desperation more completely. Propaganda is the science of making people *truly* need the same thing.

**Grief Merchants**
Since desperacy requires authentic need, there exists a dark trade in creating genuine circumstances of desperation. Grief Merchants arrange for people to lose things—truly lose them—so they can need them back. It's legal in some provinces. The ethics are exactly as horrifying as you'd expect.

**The Burnout Wards**
Hospitals for those who pulled too hard. Patients there are husks—technically alive but having spent their life-force invoking major desperacies. Some did it to save loved ones. Some were addicts of the power. All of them are warnings.

## What Is Easy, What Is Hard

**Easy:**
- Emergency response. Genuine crises practically solve themselves as desperacy warps probability and resources toward the epicenter
- Finding lost children—the parent's need is a homing beacon
- Medical care for the truly dying (their need pulls healers and medicines toward them)
- Revolution against satisfied, comfortable tyrants
- Locating water in a desert when you're truly dying of thirst

**Hard:**
- Long-term planning. You can't need something that isn't urgent
- Preventive medicine. No one desperately needs to prevent a disease they don't have
- Contentment. Satisfaction is dangerous; happiness makes you powerless
- Helping people who don't want help. Their lack of need repels assistance
- Wealth accumulation. The universe keeps redistributing toward the desperate
- Art for art's sake. Creating without need produces only faint, wispy things
- Loving someone who doesn't need you

The deepest philosophical question in this world: *Is it better to need nothing and be powerless, or to need everything and be consumed?*
