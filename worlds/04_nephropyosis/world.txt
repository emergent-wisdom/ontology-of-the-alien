# The World of Nephropyosis

## The Core Principle

In this world, **all existence is filtration**. Reality itself is a vast membrane through which everything must continuously pass. Nothing remains static—matter, energy, thought, and time are perpetually percolating through invisible barriers, separating into *essence* (what passes through) and *sediment* (what remains behind).

Nephropyosis is the fundamental law: **Every act of existence generates residue. Every passage leaves accumulation. Nothing filters cleanly.**

The universe is not made of atoms or waves, but of *flux-states*—moments of transition as substances pass through the Great Membrane that underlies all space. What we perceive as "solid matter" is simply flux that has temporarily slowed its filtration. What we call "energy" is flux moving rapidly through the membrane layers.

---

## The Laws That Emerge

### 1. The Law of Inevitable Sediment
Every process that refines, purifies, transforms, or even simply *continues existing* produces sediment. This sediment is real, physical, and cannot be destroyed—only relocated or transformed into different sediment. Burning wood doesn't eliminate it; the sediment appears as ash, smoke-residue in the upper atmosphere, and *thermal silt* that settles in cold places.

### 2. The Law of Membrane Proximity
Everything exists at a certain "depth" relative to the Great Membrane. Objects closer to the membrane filter faster, age quicker, and generate sediment rapidly. Objects further from the membrane are sluggish, preserved, almost frozen. Mountains are far from the membrane; rivers are close. This is why water flows and stone endures.

### 3. The Law of Reciprocal Passage
When something filters *through*, something else must filter *back*. The membrane maintains balance. If you purify water, somewhere else water becomes impure. If you clarify your thoughts, confusion accumulates in the spaces where you are not. Long-distance effects are diluted but never zero.

### 4. The Law of Sediment Memory
Sediment retains an impression of what it was filtered from. Ancient sediment beds contain the "memory" of primordial filtrations—the first separations of light from darkness, solidity from void. Those who know how to read sediment can perceive echoes of the past. Those who know how to *dissolve* specific sediments can briefly reverse what was filtered.

### 5. The Law of Terminal Saturation
Any bounded region can only hold so much sediment before filtration stops entirely. When an area reaches terminal saturation, nothing can change within it—a state called *lithostasis*. Ancient battlefields, sites of great industry, or places of prolonged suffering eventually become saturated and frozen outside time.

## How Society Organizes

### The Sediment Economy
Wealth in this world is measured not in gold but in **clear space**—land that has been recently purged of sediment and can support new filtration. The poorest people live in high-sediment slums where everything is sluggish, dim, and difficult. The rich maintain vast "clarity estates" by employing armies of *porters* who physically carry sediment away to designated dumping zones.

**Sediment porters** form the backbone of the labor class. They haul wheelbarrows of accumulated residue to the Deeps—designated sacrifice zones, often in valleys or underground caverns, where sediment is allowed to accumulate toward lithostasis. Entire mountain ranges have become lithostatic—frozen, unchanging, uninhabitable but also imperishable.

### The Filtration Priesthood
The **Nephric Order** are monk-scientists who study membrane proximity and sediment dynamics. They maintain the **Purity Calendars** that track regional sediment levels and predict saturation events. They also perform **translocation rituals**—carefully balanced filtrations that move sediment from critical areas (hospitals, granaries, nurseries) to sacrifice zones.

Their greatest secret: they know locations where the membrane is *torn*, allowing filtration without sediment. These **clear passages** are zealously guarded and used only for the most sacred purposes.

### Guilds and Professions

- **Clarifiers**: Artisans who create refined goods—clear glass, pure metals, distilled spirits. They are wealthy but must pay heavy sediment-taxes, as their work generates enormous residue.
- **Muddlers**: The opposite profession. They deliberately contaminate and mix, producing useful sediment-absorbing compounds that can be used to "pre-saturate" areas, making them safe to abandon.
- **Readers**: Those who interpret sediment memory. Historians, detectives, and prophets (since future-filtration casts backward sediment-shadows).
- **Deep-walkers**: Explorers of lithostatic zones. Time does not pass there, so they train to hold their breath, move without filtering, and retrieve artifacts frozen in ancient saturation events.

### Architecture and Cities
Buildings are designed with **sediment channels**—gutters, drains, and flues that direct accumulating residue away from living spaces and toward collection points. Poor neighborhoods have clogged channels; wealthy districts have elaborate sediment-plumbing maintained by guild engineers.

Cities are built on slopes whenever possible, so sediment flows downhill naturally. The lowest districts are called **siltwells** and are inhabited by the desperate, the criminal, and the strange—those who have adapted to high-sediment existence.

## What Is Easy / What Is Hard

### Easy in This World:
- **Preservation**: Place something far from the membrane (deep underground, inside lead-lined vaults, at mountain peaks) and it barely ages. Museums are built in former lithostatic zones.
- **Detection**: Everything leaves sediment traces. Tracking someone, identifying what happened in a place, proving someone did something—all trivially easy by reading residue.
- **Stability**: Once something is established and sediment-balanced, it tends to persist. Traditions endure. Institutions calcify. Revolutions are difficult because change generates massive sediment.
- **Understanding the past**: Sediment memory makes history tangible, readable, almost irrefutable.

### Hard in This World:
- **Innovation**: Every new process generates unprecedented sediment in unpredictable patterns. Inventors are regarded as dangerous, potentially catastrophic figures.
- **Secrecy**: You cannot act without leaving filtration traces. Privacy is nearly impossible. The Nephric Order knows almost everything that happens.
- **Healing**: Curing disease means filtering corruption from the body—which generates internal sediment. Surgery is brutal; the patient often trades one ailment for sediment-sickness.
- **Equality**: Clear space is finite. Someone must always live in the accumulated residue of others' refinement. The system is inherently extractive.
- **Moving on**: Grief, trauma, anger—emotional filtration leaves sediment in the psyche. The past literally weighs on you. Some people become lithostatic themselves, frozen in their accumulated sorrows.
- **Forgetting**: Sediment remembers even when you don't. The things you filtered away still exist, somewhere, waiting to be read.

---

This is the world of Nephropyosis: a reality of continuous passage, inevitable accumulation, and the terrible mathematics of purity—where every clarification creates a shadow, every refinement demands a sacrifice zone, and the sediment of all history piles ever higher in the Deeps.
