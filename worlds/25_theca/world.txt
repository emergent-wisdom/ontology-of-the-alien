# The World of Theca

## The Core Principle

In this world, **nothing can exist without being contained**. Existence itself requires enclosure. The fundamental law states: *that which has no boundary has no being*. Every particle, every force, every thought must be housed within a theca—a defining membrane, shell, or case—or it dissipates into non-existence.

The universe is not made of matter and energy, but of **nested containments**. Reality is an infinite regression of sheaths within sheaths, each theca giving form and persistence to what it holds. The smallest unit of existence is not a particle but a *minimal theca*—the simplest possible boundary that can hold something distinct from the void.

## The Emergent Laws

### 1. The Law of Necessary Enclosure
Nothing persists uncontained. Water left in open air doesn't evaporate—it *unbecomings*, fading from reality entirely. Sound without a resonating chamber ceases. Even light must travel through transparent thecae (crystalline tubes that web the atmosphere) or it simply stops being.

### 2. The Law of Thecal Inheritance
A theca can only contain things whose nature is compatible with its own boundary-substance. A clay vessel can hold water and grain but not fire. A glass theca can hold light but not shadow. This isn't about physical damage—incompatible contents simply pass through or are rejected, remaining uncontained and thus unreal within that context.

### 3. The Law of Boundary Pressure
The strength of something's existence is proportional to how many thecae contain it. A thought held only in one mind is fragile. The same thought written in a book (paper theca), stored in a library (architectural theca), within a city (wall theca) gains ontological weight. Multiply-contained things are more *real* than singly-contained things.

### 4. The Law of Thecal Sacrifice
To release something from its theca is to risk its annihilation, but also to enable transformation. A seed must shed its coat to become a plant. A child must leave the womb. The moment of boundary-crossing is called the *naked interval*—a terrifying instant when existence hangs uncertain. Master Thecalists can extend or shorten this interval.

### 5. The Law of Recursive Depth
The outermost theca of any system determines what can exist within all inner thecae. If a kingdom's walls are made of stone, nothing incompatible with stone's nature can persist anywhere inside—not in houses, not in boxes, not in pockets. This is why nations guard their outer boundaries with obsessive care.

## Society and Daily Life

### Architecture and Dwelling
Homes are not shelters from weather but **existence-anchors**. To be homeless is to be ontologically precarious—the unhoused gradually become translucent, their voices harder to hear, their footprints shallower. Cities are built as concentric rings of walls, each ring adding another layer of reality to those within. The innermost districts, wrapped in seven or eight nested walls, are where the most important things are kept: libraries, treasuries, the dying who must not yet pass.

### Professions
**Thecawrights** are the most respected craftspeople—they build containers of all kinds, from simple ceramic jars to complex nested reliquaries. Their apprenticeship takes decades because understanding what a boundary *is* requires both physical skill and philosophical depth.

**Boundarists** are scholars who study thecal compatibility. Before any important object is stored, a Boundarist must be consulted to ensure the container's nature won't reject or corrupt the contents.

**Naked-Walkers** are rare individuals who have trained to extend their naked interval indefinitely. They can exist briefly without any theca at all—terrifying, ghostlike figures who serve as messengers between incompatible zones, or as assassins who can pass through any wall.

### Economy and Trade
Value is measured partly in **thecal depth**. A coin kept in a purse, in a strongbox, in a vault, in a fortress is worth more than the same coin loose in a pocket. Merchants don't just sell goods—they sell goods *plus their containment history*. A spice that has been kept in seventeen nested containers since harvest commands premium prices.

**Thecal banking** involves storing your valuables in increasingly nested containers maintained by trusted institutions. Withdrawing something means carefully extracting it through each layer—a process that can take days for deeply stored items.

### Family and Relationships
Children are considered to still be within their mother's theca until a formal "releasing ceremony" at age seven, even though they physically departed the womb at birth. Until then, they cannot legally own property (they *are* property, technically contained). Marriage creates a "mutual theca"—a metaphysical boundary that contains both partners, making their fates and realities interlinked.

### Death and the Afterlife
The dying are wrapped in layer after layer of ceremonial cloth, placed in nested coffins, buried in walled cemeteries. This isn't ritual—it's necessity. A soul released without sufficient containment simply ceases. The wealthy are buried in seven-layered tombs; the poor sometimes share communal boundary-walls, their individual existence becoming fuzzy at the edges, blending with neighbors.

## What Is Easy

- **Preservation**: Anything properly contained lasts indefinitely. There is no decay within a well-made theca, only stasis. Ancient foods, perfectly contained, are eaten at royal feasts.
- **Security**: Theft requires not just taking something but providing it continuous containment during extraction. Most thieves fail at the naked interval.
- **Privacy**: Thoughts contained only within the skull-theca cannot be accessed by anyone. True secrets are perfectly safe.
- **Categorization**: Everything must be put somewhere. The world is naturally organized, sortable, comprehensible in its nested structure.

## What Is Hard

- **Freedom of movement**: Every journey means repeatedly shedding and entering thecae—leaving your house, passing through city gates, entering another's dwelling. Each transition carries risk.
- **Innovation**: New ideas must find compatible containers. A thought too radical for existing thecal structures may simply fail to persist long enough to be communicated.
- **Equality**: The poor, living in thin-walled dwellings or no dwellings at all, are literally *less real* than the wealthy in their nested mansions. This isn't metaphor—their voices are quieter, their footsteps leave lighter impressions, their children are more likely to fade.
- **Wilderness**: The lands beyond all walls are called the Uncontained. Few things exist there—only creatures who have evolved their own self-thecae (shells, carapaces, thick hides) can survive. Forests are impossible; each tree would need its own boundary.
- **Revolution**: To overthrow the outer walls is to risk everything within them becoming unreal. Even tyrants are tolerated because the alternative—a naked interval for an entire population—is unthinkable.

---

This is the world of Theca: a universe of boxes within boxes, where to be is to be held, and freedom is the most dangerous thing imaginable.
